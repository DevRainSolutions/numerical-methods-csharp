   In numerical analysis, the Runge–Kutta methods (German pronunciation: [ˌʀʊŋəˈkʊta]) are an important family of implicit and explicit iterative methods for the approximation of solutions of ordinary differential equations. These techniques were developed around 1900 by the German mathematicians C. Runge and M.W. Kutta.

   This method is one of the most commonly used methods of integrating differential equations. This method is used so widely that in literature it is simply called "the Runge-Kutta method" without any indication of the type or order. This classic method is described by the following five ratios:

      y[m+1] = y[m] + h*(k[1] + 2*k[2] + 2*k[3] + k[4])/6

      k[1] = f(x[m], y[m])

      k[2] = f(x[m]+h/2, y[m]+h*k[1]/2)

      k[3] = f(x[m]+h/2, y[m]+h*k[2]/2)

      k[4] = f(x[m]+h, y[m]+h*k[3])
